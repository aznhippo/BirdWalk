Brewer Road Area to the West of Lincoln

This unassuming and unsung area holds some of the largest raptor concentrations in the region.  It can also host immense flocks of Snow Geese, Greater White-fronted Geese, and Tundra Swans.  When water conditions are right, the shorebirding can be very good.  It has consistently produced longspurs among the Horned Lark flocks.  The Lincoln Christmas Bird Count, begun in 2002, is documenting the area’s avian riches, while much of the area is under heavy development pressure.

Additional information is available at the Birding Hotspots of Placer County Web site (geocities.com/placerbird/BirdingBealsPoint.htm) by Bruce Webb.

Birding the site

A good place to start is on Brewer Road at the junction of Nicolaus Road.  Look for Horned Lark flocks along Brewer Road in the first mile north from the junction.  Knowing the longspurs’ calls is very helpful.  Chestnut-collared, Lapland, and McCown’s Longspurs have been found here (late fall through early spring).  The first Ruff and Glossy Ibis for Placer County were reported here.

Heading north on Brewer Road from Nicolaus Road, it is 2.0 miles north to Wise Road,  2.6 miles to Hicks Road, 3.0 miles to Waltz Road, and 4.0 miles to Kempton Road.  There is a nice bit of riparian at Hicks Road.  Wise and Waltz Roads both go east for 4.0 miles to the north/south running Dowd Road.  Kempton Road makes its way east to Placer Road, which goes north to both Dalby and Riosa Roads; both of these lead to Dowd Road.  Pleasant Grove Road, to the west, runs parallel with Brewer Road.  It is worth checking, though the traffic is often heavy.

Raptors, including Bald Eagles, Ferruginous Hawks, Rough-legged Hawks (variable year to year), Prairie Falcons, and Merlins are regularly recorded in winter.  Short-eared Owls may be found at dusk—recently a good number were found on Waltz Road (1.5 miles east of Brewer Road, west of the Placer/Sutter County line).  Depending on water levels, check promising fields for shorebirds and waterfowl.  Careful scrutiny of Tundra Swan flocks might produce Trumpeter Swans or Bewick’s Swans.  Also, check the sparrow flocks.  There can be astonishing numbers of Savannah Sparrows and White-crowned Sparrows, and the occasional Vesper Sparrow is found.  Tricolored Blackbirds are usually among the blackbird flocks in winter.

Bruce Webb suggests checking Moore Road where it crosses Auburn Ravine Creek (1.0 mile east of Dowd Road, or 5.0 miles east of Brewer Road).  Moore Road is 2.0 miles south of Nicolaus Road.  Least Flycatcher and Blackpoll Warbler have been found there.
